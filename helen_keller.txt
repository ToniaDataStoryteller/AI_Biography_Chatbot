Helen Keller was an American author and educator born in June 1880 in Tuscumbia, Alabama. 
At nineteen months old, she contracted an illness that left her both deaf and blind.
Anne Sullivan became her famous teacher and life-long companion in 1887.
Anne Sullivan taught Helen the word for water by pouring it over her hand while spelling 'w-a-t-e-r' into her palm.
Helen Keller learned to speak by feeling the vibrations of her teacher's throat and lips.
She was the first deaf-blind person to earn a Bachelor of Arts degree from Radcliffe College.
Alexander Graham Bell was a close friend who helped her find her first teacher.
Mark Twain was another famous friend who admired her courage and intelligence.
Helen Keller was a co-founder of the American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU).
She traveled to over 35 countries to advocate for people with disabilities.
Her autobiography is titled The Story of My Life and was published in 1903.
Helen Keller died in June 1968, leaving behind a legacy of activism and hope.
